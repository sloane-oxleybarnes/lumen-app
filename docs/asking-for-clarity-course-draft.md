**Status:** Current beta course text  
**Section:** Professional - Colleague

## 1. What Clarity Actually Is

**Format:** Accordion

**Description:**  
Clarity is shared alignment, not needing extra help.

Asking for clarity at work should be simple, but, for many neurodivergent people it's not. This course covers why asking for clarity can be so difficult, how to identify the information you need, and how to ask in a way that is direct and confident.

### Why ask for clarification? 

A clear understanding of what's asked of you and how to execute on it ensures that you're solving the right problem at the right level before you spend your energy on it.

### What can be unclear? 

When something is asked of us there are many factors that dictate what is actually needed: Timeline (deadline & priority), definition of done, the audience and who the decision maker is are a few.

## 2. Why Asking Can Feel Hard

**Format:** Select-multiple reflection

**Description:**  
Asking for clarification can be harder than it should be. Not because you don't understand or can't do the work — but because of everything that surrounds it. 
- The worry that you should already know. Not being sure what information you are even missing. 
- The sense that you are asking too much, taking too long, being annoying. 
- The impulse to apologize before you have said anything at all.

These are not character flaws. They are thought patterns — and these can be changed.

**Prompt:**  
What concerns come up for you?

**Options:**

- Worrying that I should already know
- Not knowing what information I need
- Fear of sounding annoying
- Needing more specificity than others
- Knowing that I process spoken instructions slowly
- Feeling guilty for having to ask

## 3. Lets Talk About Over-Apologizing

**Format:** Spot the difference (Add click to flip over on the cards)

**Description:**  
Many neurodivergent people grow up feeling like their questions are a burden — being told to stop asking, to just figure it out, or that they should already know. If that sounds familiar, it makes sense that asking for help now feels like something you need to apologize for. It is not (bold). 

Asking clarifying questions is not a weakness. It is one of the clearest, most direct ways to communicate - and you don't need to apologize for it. This section will help you notice when you are over-apologizing and give you tools to ask for what you need without shrinking yourself to do it.

**Scenario:**  
You need to ask what "clean this up" means.

### Weaker version

Sorry, this is probably annoying, but can you explain what you meant by clean this up?

**Why it is weaker:**  
The apology makes you seem less confident.

### Stronger version

Quick clarification: when you say clean this up, do you mean copy edits, structure, or both? 

**Why it is stronger:**  
Specific, neutral, and easy to answer.

## 5. The Clarity Formula

**Format:** Visual formula

**Description:**  
Knowing you need clarification and knowing how and what to ask for it are two different things. This formula bridges that gap — giving your brain a clear structure to work from so asking feels less overwhelming.

### Step 1: What I understand

Start with what you think is true.

Example: I understand the goal is to finalize the onboarding process by next Friday.

### Step 2: What is unclear

Name the missing piece.

Example: I am not sure what need to be finalized: the copy, format, or something else.

### Step 3: Specific question

Ask an answerable question.

Example: Should I prioritize the copy or the format?

### Step 4: Why it helps

Connect it to an improved outcome.

Example: That will ensure I deliver this on time and to the right standards.

**Format:** Flip cards

### Audience

Question: Will this be an internal or external facing document? 

### Decision Maker

Who needs to approve the final version?

Question: Will this be reviewed by the team or should I send it to someone specific?

## 7. Step 1 - What Do I Know?

**Description:**
The first step in the Clarity Formula is identifying what you already know. Say what you think is true. It anchors the question, shows you have been listening, and gives the other person a clear starting point to correct or confirm.

**Format:** Multiple choice with Message Prompt

**Instructions:**
Read the message and select all the things you can confirm you already know. Select all that apply.

### Round 1

Message from Jordan, your manager:

Hey — can you take a look at the onboarding doc and clean it up before the team review on Thursday?

Question:

What do you already know from this message? Select all that apply.


**Correct answers:**  
- There is a team review happening on Thursday
- The onboarding doc is the one that needs work
- The work needs to be done before Thursday

**Why:**  
You already have more than you think. You know the document, the deadline, and the event it is for. What is missing is the definition of done. 

**Incorrect options:**

- Whether anyone need to approve the updates before the team review
- How polished the final version needs to be
- The specific parts of the document need to be "cleaned up"
- Whether anyone else is also working on the doc

### Round 2

Slack message from a teammate:

Can you sent the client email for the Halford account? I have a conflict this afternoon.
Question:

What do you already know from this message? Select all that apply.


**Correct answers:**  
- There is a client email that needs to be sent
- Your teammate cannot handle it this afternoon
- The account is Halford

**Why:**  
You know you need to send an email and why your teammate cannot do it. What is missing is the timing, content, audience, and whether there is a draft started.

**Incorrect options:**

- When the email needs to go out
- What the email should say or what the goal is
- Whether there is a draft already started
- Who the email is going to

### Round 3

Email from a senior colleague:

I would love your input on the new process doc before it goes to leadership. Let me know what you think when you get a chance.
Question:

What do you already know from this message? Select all that apply.


**Correct answers:**  
- There is a process doc that is being finalized
- It is going to leadership at some point
- They want your input before it goes out

**Why:**  
You know the document exists, where it is headed, and that your input is wanted. But when you get a chance could mean today or next week — and let me know what you think could mean anything from a gut reaction to a line-by-line edit. Starting with what you do know makes it much easier to name those gaps without the question feeling vague or demanding.

**Incorrect options:**

- What kind of feedback they are looking for
- How detailed or thorough your review should be
- When they need your response by
- Whether this is urgent or low priority

## 8. Step 2 - What Information Is Missing?

**Description:)**
Not everything that feels unclear actually is — usually there is a primary piece of information missing that is creating the confusion. Name that, and the question almost writes itself.

**Format:** Multiple choice

### Round 1

Your manager says, "Can you clean up the onboarding doc?" 

What information is most missing?

**Correct answer:**  
Definition of done: what clean up means.

**Why:**  
You need to know whether this means copy, format, polish, or all of it.

**Incorrect options:**

- The order you should tackle each section
- Whether to send it when you are done or wait for a review meeting

### Round 2

A teammate says, "Can you help with the launch issue?" 

What information is most missing?

**Correct answer:**  
Ownership and urgency.

**Why:**  
You need to know what part they want you to own and whether this is urgent.

**Incorrect options:**

- Whether this is related to the bug reported last week
- Who else is already working on it


### Round 3

Your skip-level manager sends you a message that says "Can you take a look at the new process doc and let me know what you think?" What the most important missing information?

**Correct answer:**  
Scope and purpose

**Why:**  
"Let me know what you think" could mean anything from a gut reaction to a detailed line edit to a strategic critique. Without knowing what kind of input they want, you could spend an hour on deep structural feedback when they just wanted a quick read for tone — or vice versa.
**Incorrect options:**

- Whether they wrote the doc themselves or inherited it from someone else
- How long the doc is and whether you have time to read it today

## 9. Step 3 - How to Ask

**Description:**
The third step in the Clarity Formula is making sure the questions you ask are specific and not vague. hA question like "can you clarify?" puts the work back on them. A question like "do you need this by end of day or can it wait until end of week?" gives them something to respond to immediately — and gets you what you need faster.

**Format:** Sorting activity

**Instruction:**  
Sort each message by the type of question. 

**Categories:**

- Strong clarity question
- Too vague
- Too apologetic

### Message 1

Can you clarify?

**Answer:** Too vague  
**Why:** It does not say what part needs clarification.

### Message 2

When you say urgent, do you mean today or before Friday?

**Answer:** Strong clarity question  
**Why:** Names the unclear word and gives two answerable options.

### Message 3

Sorry, I know this is probably obvious, but I am confused about what you want.

**Answer:** Too apologetic  
**Why:** The apology takes up more space than the question.

### Message 4

Should I focus first on the headline copy or the form steps?

**Answer:** Strong clarity question  
**Why:** Specific and easy to answer.

## 10. Step 4 - Why This Helps You

**Description:**
The last step in the Clarity Formula is explaining why this information is important and how it improves the shared outcome. It signals that you are thinking critically and want to deliver the best work. That changes how it lands.

**Instruction:**  
Drag each closing line into the correct column — connects to the work or does not connect to the work.

**Format:** Drag and Drop Activity

**Categories:**

- Connects to the work
- Does not connect to the work

### Item 1

That will help me avoid redoing it after the review.

**Answer:** Connects to the work 
**Why:** It names a specific outcome — avoiding rework — that benefits the project, not just the person asking.

### Item 2

I just want to make sure I am not doing it wrong.

**Answer:** Does not connect to the work
**Why:** This frames the question around personal anxiety rather than a work outcome. It puts the focus on the asker's fear rather than the task.

### Item 3

That will help me prioritize correctly before I start.

**Answer:** Connects to the work
**Why:** It links the clarification directly to a decision that affects the quality and direction of the work.

### Item 4

I always get confused by this kind of thing.

**Answer:** Does not connect to the work
**Why:** This explains the asker's pattern rather than connecting the question to the task at hand. It invites sympathy rather than a useful answer.

### Item 5

Knowing this will help me scope the work accurately before the deadline.

**Answer:** Connects to the work
**Why:** It connects the missing information to a concrete work outcome — accurate scoping — that the other person also cares about.

### Item 6

I just do not want to get it wrong and have to redo everything.

**Answer:** Does not connect to the work
**Why:** While the concern is valid, the framing centers fear of failure rather than the benefit to the work. A small reframe — that will help me avoid rework — says the same thing from a work-focused angle.

### Item 7

That will help me make sure the output is at the right level before I hand it off.

**Answer:** Connects to the work
**Why:** It names a specific checkpoint — handing off at the right level — that is about the work, not the asker's comfort.

### Item 8

I feel like I missed something in the briefing.

**Answer:** Does not connect to the work
**Why:** This explains a feeling rather than connecting the question to an outcome. It may also undermine confidence unnecessarily.

## 10. Put It All Together

**Format:** Multiple choice — select the best response from three options

**Tips icon:** 
A small circular icon with a lightbulb or question mark sits in the top right corner of every round. When the user hovers over or taps it, a tooltip appears showing the Clarity Checklist. The checklist is framed in third person since the activity is evaluating someone else's response, not the user's own writing.

Tooltip — Clarity Checklist:

Use this to evaluate each response before selecting.

The response names what is already understood
The response identifies the specific unclear part
The response asks one specific answerable question
The response gives options where that makes answering easier
The response does not include an apology for needing the information

Tooltip design notes:

The icon should be small and unobtrusive — 20px circle in amber with a white lightbulb or question mark inside
On desktop: tooltip appears on hover, positioned above or to the left of the icon so it does not obscure the answer options
On mobile: tooltip appears on tap, dismisses on tap outside
The checklist items should use a small checkbox or bullet in amber
The tooltip should have a light background with a subtle border — consistent with the rest of the course UI
The icon and tooltip should appear on every round of the activity, not just the first one

**Description:**  
You have learned what clarity is, why asking for it feels hard, and how to build a question that actually works. Now it is time to use everything at once. 

Each round below shows a real workplace message and three possible responses. Only one response puts the full Clarity Formula together correctly — it grounds itself in what is already known, names the specific gap, asks an answerable question, and connects it to the work. The other two are close but fall short in ways that matter. 

Read carefully before you choose.

**Instructions:**
Read the message and select the response that best applies the Clarity Formula. Think about what each option gets right and what it is missing before you choose.

### Round 1:

Message from your manager, sent Monday morning:

Hey — before you start on the homepage copy, just make sure it matches the new direction we talked about.

**Correct answer:**  
I have my notes from our last conversation. I am not sure whether the new direction means a different tone, structure, or messaging priorities. Which should I focus on first so I avoid rewriting anything after the fact?

**Why:**  
It names what is already known (the notes from the last conversation), identifies the specific gap with three clear options (tone, structure, or messaging priorities), asks one answerable question, and connects it to avoiding rework. Every step of the formula is present.

**Incorrect options:**

- I have my notes from our last conversation. Before I start, I want to confirm — when you say new direction, are you referring to the Friday meeting or something more recent?
- I want to make sure I get this right before I start. Could you point me to where the new direction is documented so I am working from the right version?

### Round 2:

Slack message from a senior colleague:

Can you take a look at the deck before it goes to the client and just tighten it up a bit?

**Correct answer:**  
I know the presentation is going out soon. To make sure I focus on what matters most — do you mean tightening the copy, cutting slides, or improving visual consistency?

**Why:**  
It names what is already known (the presentation is going out soon), identifies the specific gap precisely, and gives three clear options that make the question immediately answerable. It is efficient and uses the formula without anything missing.

**Incorrect options:**

- Happy to help before it goes to the client. Should I track my changes so you can review what I touched before it goes out?
- I know the deck is mostly finalized. I want to flag that I can focus on either the copy or the visual side — which would be more useful for the client version?

### Round 3:

Email from a project lead you have not worked with before:

We need someone to own the onboarding section of the handbook. Let me know if you can take it on.

**Correct answer:**  
I know this is for the new team handbook. Before I commit, I want to clarify — does own mean writing from scratch, editing an existing draft, or coordinating input from others? That will help me give you a realistic timeline.

**Why:**  
It names what is already known (this is for the new team handbook), identifies the single most critical gap (what own means), gives three concrete options, and connects it to a realistic timeline. The formula is complete and the question is immediately actionable.

**Incorrect options:**

- I would be glad to take this on. Before I commit, could you share any existing drafts or briefs so I know what I am working with?
- Happy to help with the onboarding section. I want to make sure I scope it correctly — is this just the first week or does it cover the full first month of joining?

### Round 4:

Message from your manager, end of day Friday:

Can you pull together a summary of where we are on the project for the leadership update next week?

**Correct answer:**  
I know the project has three active workstreams. Should the summary cover all three or just the ones with recent movement? That will help me make sure it is scoped correctly before I start drafting.

**Why:**  
It names what is already known (the three active workstreams), identifies the most critical gap (scope — which workstreams to include), asks one specific answerable question, and connects it to starting correctly. The formula is complete and the question directly affects how the work gets done.

**Incorrect options:**

- I know the project has three active workstreams. Should the summary cover all three or just the ones with recent movement? That will help me make sure it is scoped correctly before I start drafting.
- I know leadership updates usually go out Wednesday. To confirm before I start — is this the same format as last quarter's update or is there a new template I should use?

### Roleplay behavior
