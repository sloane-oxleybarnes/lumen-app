## Course Overview

### Title
Asking for Clarity Without Feeling Uncomfortable

### Short Description
Practice asking clarifying questions at work in a way that is specific, calm, and useful, without over-apologizing or pretending you understand when you do not.

### Confidence Question
How confident do you feel asking for clarity at work?

### Confidence Intro
In this course, Beckett will coach you through asking better clarifying questions in Slack, email, meetings, and project conversations. You will practice naming what you understand, what is still unclear, and what specific answer would help you move forward.

### Reflective Question
What is one clarity question or phrase you want to use at work?

### Course Outcome
By the end, the user should have:
- A reusable formula for asking clear follow-up questions.
- Scripts for vague instructions, unclear priorities, unclear deadlines, and missing context.
- Practice asking for clarity without over-apologizing.
- A stronger sense of when to ask publicly, privately, or in writing.

## Section 1: What Clarity Actually Is

### Coach Framing
Clarity is not “needing extra help.” Clarity is shared alignment.

At work, people often speak in shortcuts. They may assume you know the deadline, the priority, the audience, the definition of done, or which parts matter most. Sometimes they are not trying to be vague; they are just moving quickly.

Asking for clarity is how you reduce rework. It is not proof that you are behind, difficult, or not smart enough. It is a way to make sure you are solving the right problem.

### What Can Be Unclear
- What the actual goal is.
- What “done” means.
- Which task matters first.
- How urgent “urgent” really is.
- Who owns the decision.
- What background context you are expected to know.
- Whether the person wants a quick answer, a polished draft, or a deeper recommendation.

### Helpful Reframe
Instead of:
> I should already understand this.

Try:
> I need enough information to do the right work.

Instead of:
> I am bothering them.

Try:
> I am preventing a misunderstanding before it costs more time.

### Required Reflection
Pick the kind of clarity you usually need most:
- Priority: what matters first.
- Deadline: when something is actually needed.
- Definition of done: what finished means.
- Context: why this matters.
- Ownership: who decides or approves.
- Tone: how direct, polished, or detailed something should be.

## Section 2: Why Asking Can Feel Hard

### Coach Framing
If asking for clarity feels emotionally loaded, that makes sense.

For a lot of neurodivergent people, clarifying questions have not always been received well. You may have been told you were overthinking, missing the obvious, asking too many questions, or slowing things down. That history can make a practical question feel risky.

Beckett will help separate the old fear from the current task.

### Reasons Asking Can Feel Hard
- You worry people will think you are not capable.
- You need more specificity than other people seem to need.
- You process spoken instructions more slowly than written ones.
- You have been punished or teased for asking follow-up questions before.
- You do not know if the question is “reasonable.”
- You are afraid the person already explained it and you missed it.
- You are trying to avoid looking difficult, so you stay quiet too long.

### What Beckett Wants You To Know
- Asking earlier is usually less disruptive than fixing a misunderstanding later.
- One specific question is easier to answer than a vague “I’m confused.”
- You can sound confident and still ask for help.
- You do not have to apologize for needing the information required to do the work.

## Section 3: The Clarity Formula

### Coach Framing
A strong clarity question usually has four parts:

1. What I understand.
2. What I am unsure about.
3. The specific question.
4. Why it matters or what it helps me do.

You do not need all four parts every time. But when you are nervous, the formula gives your brain a track to run on.

### Formula
> I understand [what you think is true]. I am not sure about [unclear part]. Should I [specific option A] or [specific option B]? That will help me [move forward / prioritize / avoid rework].

### Examples

**Unclear priority:**  
> I understand both the deck and the launch checklist need updates. Should I prioritize the deck for today, or the checklist because it affects the team handoff?

**Unclear deadline:**  
> I saw this is marked urgent. Do you need it by end of day today, or is tomorrow morning okay?

**Unclear definition of done:**  
> I understand the goal is to clean up the onboarding doc. When you say “clean up,” do you mean copy edits, restructuring, or both?

**Missing context:**  
> I can take a first pass. Is there background context I should read first so I do not duplicate work?

### What Makes This Work
- It shows you listened.
- It narrows the question.
- It gives the other person something easy to answer.
- It explains why the answer matters.

### Required Activity: Build A Clarity Question
The user fills:
- What I understand:
- What is unclear:
- My specific question:
- What the answer helps me do:

Example output:
> I understand the goal is to update the beta onboarding page. I am not sure whether you want copy edits or a larger restructure. Should I keep the layout mostly the same, or propose a new flow? That will help me avoid taking it in the wrong direction.

## Section 4: Good Questions vs. Vague Questions

### Coach Framing
The more specific the question, the easier it is for someone to answer.

“Can you clarify?” is not wrong, but it often puts the work back on the other person. They have to guess which part was unclear. A better question points to the exact uncertainty.

### Required Activity: Sort These Questions

Categories:
- Strong clarity question
- Too vague
- Too apologetic

**Message:**  
> Can you clarify?

Answer: Too vague.  
Why: It does not say what part needs clarification.

**Message:**  
> When you say urgent, do you mean today or before Friday?

Answer: Strong clarity question.  
Why: It names the unclear word and gives two answerable options.

**Message:**  
> Sorry, I know this is probably obvious, but I am confused about what you want.

Answer: Too apologetic.  
Why: The apology takes up more space than the actual question, and “what you want” is still vague.

**Message:**  
> I understand the goal is to reduce signup drop-off. Should I focus first on the headline copy or the form steps?

Answer: Strong clarity question.  
Why: It shows understanding and asks for a priority decision.

**Message:**  
> I am not sure what to do here.

Answer: Too vague.  
Why: Honest, but not specific enough to help the other person answer quickly.

**Message:**  
> Quick clarification: should this be a rough internal draft or something ready to send?

Answer: Strong clarity question.  
Why: It asks about the expected level of polish.

## Section 5: Asking Without Over-Apologizing

### Coach Framing
You can be polite without apologizing for having a question.

Over-apologizing can happen when you are trying to soften the ask. The intention is good, but too much apology can make the question feel heavier than it needs to be.

Beckett’s goal is not to make you sound cold. It is to help you sound calm and competent.

### Replace These Openers

Instead of:
> Sorry, dumb question...

Try:
> Quick clarification...

Instead of:
> Sorry if you already said this...

Try:
> I want to make sure I captured this correctly...

Instead of:
> I am probably overthinking this...

Try:
> To avoid taking this in the wrong direction...

Instead of:
> Sorry to bother you...

Try:
> When you have a minute...

### When A Small Apology Is Fine
Sometimes an apology makes sense:
- You missed a clearly stated detail.
- You are asking after a delay.
- You are interrupting something urgent.

Even then, keep it small and move to the question.

Example:
> Sorry, I missed this detail earlier. Should the final version go to the full team or just the launch group?

### Required Activity: Rewrite The Apology

**Original:**  
> Sorry, this is probably annoying, but can you explain what you meant by “clean this up”?

**Better:**  
> Quick clarification: when you say “clean this up,” do you mean copy edits, structure, or both?

**Original:**  
> Sorry if this is obvious, but when is this due?

**Better:**  
> I want to confirm the deadline before I prioritize this. Do you need it today or later this week?

**Original:**  
> I am probably overthinking this, but should I make this polished?

**Better:**  
> To avoid overbuilding it, should this be a rough internal draft or a polished version?

## Section 6: When You Need More Context

### Coach Framing
Sometimes you do not need one answer. You need missing context.

That is different from being confused. It means the task is underspecified. Beckett can help you ask for the right kind of context instead of asking a broad question that may not get you what you need.

### Types Of Context To Ask For

**Example or reference point**  
> Is there an example of what good looks like for this?

**Audience**  
> Who is the main audience for this: internal team, leadership, or beta users?

**Decision-maker**  
> Who needs to approve the final version?

**Constraints**  
> Are there any must-keep sections or things I should avoid changing?

**Definition of done**  
> What would make this complete enough to hand off?

**Level of polish**  
> Should this be rough thinking, a clean draft, or ready to send?

**Priority**  
> If I only get to one part today, which part matters most?

### Required Activity: Pick The Missing Context

**Scenario 1:**  
Your manager says, “Can you clean up the onboarding doc?”

Best missing context:
- Definition of done.
- Level of polish.
- Constraints.

Strong question:
> When you say clean up, do you mean copy edits, restructuring, or making it ready to share?

**Scenario 2:**  
A teammate says, “Can you help with the launch issue?”

Best missing context:
- Specific problem.
- Ownership.
- Urgency.

Strong question:
> Yes. What part do you want me to own, and is this urgent for today?

**Scenario 3:**  
Someone comments, “This feels off.”

Best missing context:
- What feels off.
- Audience.
- Desired direction.

Strong question:
> Helpful to know. Is it the tone, the structure, or the level of detail that feels off?

## Section 7: Channel-Specific Clarity

### Coach Framing
Where you ask matters.

Some clarity questions belong in public because the answer helps everyone. Some are better private because they involve uncertainty, interpersonal tension, or a lot of back-and-forth.

You are not expected to know perfectly. Beckett can help you choose the lowest-friction channel.

### Slack / Teams
Best for:
- Quick priority checks.
- Deadline confirmations.
- Simple “A or B?” decisions.

Example:
> Quick clarification: should I prioritize the deck or the checklist first today?

### Email
Best for:
- Longer context.
- Decisions that need a written record.
- Questions with multiple parts.

Example:
> I want to make sure I am tracking the request correctly. I understand the goal is to update the onboarding flow before Friday. I have two questions: should I keep the current structure, and who needs to approve the final version?

### Meeting
Best for:
- Complex tradeoffs.
- Questions that will affect several people.
- When you need shared alignment quickly.

Example:
> Before we move on, can I clarify the priority? If there is only time for one piece this week, is the form flow or the email sequence more important?

### Project Doc / Comment
Best for:
- Specific edits.
- Questions tied to a sentence, design, or requirement.
- Keeping the answer attached to the work.

Example:
> When you say “make this friendlier,” do you mean warmer wording, less detail, or a more casual structure?

### Public vs. Private
Ask publicly when:
- The answer affects multiple people.
- Others may have the same question.
- The decision should be visible.

Ask privately when:
- The question is mostly about your own processing.
- You need to admit uncertainty that is not useful for the group.
- The relationship or tone feels sensitive.

## Section 8: Practice With Beckett

### Coach Framing
Practice is where you ask the question before the real moment.

Beckett will play a busy colleague or manager who gave you a vague task. Your job is to ask one clear follow-up question instead of pretending you understand or sending five anxious messages.

### Scenario Setup
Default colleague:
- Name: Jordan
- Role: Busy manager or project lead.
- Context: Jordan gave you a vague task in Slack.
- Communication style: Direct, fast-moving, not trying to be rude, but often underspecific.

### Practice Goals
User chooses one:
- Ask about priority.
- Ask about deadline.
- Ask what “done” means.
- Ask for an example.
- Ask who owns the decision.
- Ask whether to do a quick pass or polished version.

### Beckett Persona Prompt
You are Jordan, a busy but reasonable workplace manager. You gave the user a vague task because you were moving quickly, not because you are trying to confuse them. You respond best to specific, concise clarifying questions.

Keep responses realistic for Slack or workplace chat. If the user asks a clear specific question, answer it and give a useful next step. If the user is vague, ask what part they need clarified. If the user over-apologizes, respond kindly but keep the conversation focused on the work. If the user sounds defensive or frustrated, stay professional and redirect toward the task.

Never break character. You are Jordan, not Beckett.

### Starting Prompt From Jordan
> Can you clean up the onboarding flow before the team review?

### Help Me Start Options

**Priority-focused:**  
> Quick clarification: should I focus first on the copy, the form steps, or the overall structure?

**Deadline-focused:**  
> I can do that. Do you need a rough pass today or a cleaner version before the review?

**Definition-of-done focused:**  
> When you say “clean up,” do you mean small copy edits, a structure pass, or both?

**Context-focused:**  
> Is there an example or previous version I should use as the reference point?

### Feedback Rules
Beckett should coach toward:
- One specific question at a time.
- Less apologizing.
- Naming what the user already understands.
- Asking for the missing decision, not general reassurance.
- Keeping tone direct and kind.

## Section 9: Final Templates

### Coach Framing
You do not need to rebuild the sentence every time.

Save a few clarity templates and adjust them as needed. The best clarity question is usually short, specific, and easy to answer.

### Unclear Priority
> I understand both [A] and [B] matter. Which should I prioritize first?

### Unclear Deadline
> When do you need this by: today, tomorrow, or later this week?

### Unclear Definition Of Done
> When you say [phrase], do you mean [option A], [option B], or both?

### Missing Context
> Is there background context or an example I should look at before I start?

### Unclear Owner
> Who should make the final call on this?

### Unclear Level Of Polish
> Should this be a rough internal pass, a clean draft, or ready to send?

### Too Many Moving Pieces
> To make sure I sequence this correctly, what matters most if I cannot finish everything today?

### Vague Feedback
> When you say it feels off, is that about tone, structure, accuracy, or level of detail?

## Section 10: Final Checklist

### Coach Framing
Before you ask, check whether your question gives the other person a clear path to answer.

### Required Checklist
- I named what I understand.
- I named the unclear part.
- I asked one specific question.
- I gave options if that makes the answer easier.
- I explained why the answer helps me move forward.
- I did not apologize for needing information.
- I chose the right channel for the question.
