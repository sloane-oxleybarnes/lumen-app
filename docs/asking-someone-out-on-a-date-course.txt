## Slide 1: Asking Someone Out on a Date

Type:
Interactive read

Description:
Dating can be especially challenging when you're neurodivergent. Many common dating expectations rely on reading subtle social cues, interpreting ambiguity, and knowing unwritten rules that are rarely explained directly.

This course is about moving from interest to a respectful next step.

You are not learning how to impress someone, say the perfect thing, or guarantee a "yes." You are practicing how to communicate interest clearly, respectfully, and in a way that gives the other person an easy choice.

Section: What you are practicing
- Not becoming smoother, more charming, or more persuasive.
- Not trying to force certainty from an unclear situation.
- Recognizing when there is enough interest to make an invitation.
- Asking for a specific, low-pressure first meetup.

Section: The goal
By the end of this section, you will be able to:

- Decide whether someone seems like a reasonable person to ask.
- Choose a first-date option that feels comfortable, safe, and realistic.
- Write an invitation that is clear, direct, and easy to answer.

## Slide 2: Online Dating Reality Checks

Type:
Read-through

Intro:
Before we get into asking people out, it is worth spending a few minutes on online dating.

Meeting someone through an app is now common, but it works differently from meeting someone through friends, work, school, hobbies, or everyday life. Text-based conversations create different challenges: it is harder to read tone, easier to misunderstand intent, and more important to think about safety and boundaries.

The same principles from this course still apply—clear communication, low pressure, and respect for the other person's choices—but there are a few extra considerations that can make online dating safer and less confusing.

Bullets:
- Tone is harder to read over text, so clear words matter.
- You never owe photos, private information, or a fast meetup.
- Assume anything you send can be screenshotted.
- First meetings should be public and easy to leave.
- A quick video call first is a reasonable option.
- Dating apps can be draining; it is okay to limit conversations and take breaks.

## Slide 3: Neurodivergent Dating Strengths

Type:
Flip cards

Description:
A lot of dating advice focuses on fixing weaknesses. This course takes a different approach.

Many neurodivergent people bring strengths to dating that are genuinely valuable in relationships: honesty, curiosity, loyalty, depth, and the ability to notice patterns that others miss. These strengths do not guarantee success, and they may not describe you personally. But they are common enough to be worth recognizing.

The goal is not to change who you are. The goal is to use your strengths in ways that help connection grow while avoiding the situations where those same strengths can accidentally create pressure, confusion, or overwhelm.

Card: Honesty
- Saying what you mean can build trust.
- Clear interest is often kinder than vague hints.

Card: Depth
- You may be good at real conversation once the surface layer passes.
- Depth is a strength when it is paced.

Card: Pattern noticing
- You may notice consistency, effort, or mismatch over time.
- Beckett helps you sort signal from anxiety.

Card: Loyalty
- Strong care can be beautiful.
- The skill is pacing care before there is mutual trust.

## Slide 4: Why Dating Can Feel Hard

Type:
Accordion

Description:
Dating can be difficult for anyone, but some challenges show up more often for neurodivergent people.

Many dating norms rely on ambiguity, unwritten rules, and interpreting subtle social signals. At the same time, strong interest, rejection sensitivity, masking, sensory needs, and concerns about safety can add extra layers of complexity.

None of these challenges mean you are bad at dating. They simply mean you may need a clearer process than the one most dating advice assumes. This course focuses on making dating more understandable: noticing real signals, pacing connection, communicating directly, and choosing situations that support both comfort and safety.

Section: Unclear signals
- Text removes tone, facial expression, and timing context.
- Slow replies can mean many things, not just rejection.

Section: Rejection sensitivity
- A simple ask can feel like a whole verdict on your worth.
- The course treats a no as information, not proof that you failed.

Section: Pacing new interest
- Real excitement can become too much too fast.
- The goal is warm interest plus one next step.

Section: Masking and over-editing
- Trying to sound effortless can drain you.
- Prepared language is not fake; it is support.

Section: Safety and sensory load
- First meetups work better in public, lower-pressure places.
- Choose a setting where you can hear, regulate, and leave easily.

## Slide 5: Is This Person A Good Fit To Ask Out?

Type:
Flip cards

Description:
Before you think about how to ask someone out, it helps to ask a different question first: does this connection seem worth moving forward?

Interest alone is not always enough. A good first date is more likely when there are signs of mutual effort, basic compatibility, and respect for boundaries. You are not trying to predict the future or guarantee a yes. You are simply looking for enough evidence that asking is reasonable and that spending more time together could be worthwhile.

This section will help you look for signs of interest, compatibility, and safety before taking the next step.

Card: Green flags
- They make consistent effort.
- They ask questions back.
- They respect pacing.
- They make concrete plans easier, not harder.

Card: Compatibility signs
- Some overlap in routines or interests.
- A communication style that does not make you feel constantly unsafe.
- Similar availability for dating right now.

Card: Reasons to pause
- They pressure you for photos or private information.
- They ignore boundaries.
- They leave you carrying the entire conversation.

## Slide 7: The Clear Ask Formula

Type:
Visual formula

Description:
Asking someone out can feel intimidating, especially when you're trying to figure out the "right" thing to say.

The good news is that a first-date invitation does not need to be clever, perfect, or highly romantic. In most cases, it only needs three things: a genuine signal of interest, a specific suggestion, and room for the other person to choose freely.

This formula gives you a simple structure you can use when your brain is overthinking, anxious, or getting stuck on wording. The goal is not to guarantee a yes. The goal is to make your interest clear and make it easy for the other person to respond honestly.

Step 1: Warm signal
Text: Name the connection or thing you enjoyed.
Example: I have liked talking with you about music.

Step 2: Specific plan
Text: Suggest one concrete public plan.
Example: Would you want to grab coffee Saturday afternoon?

Step 3: Easy out
Text: Make it clear they can say no.
Example: No pressure if not.

## Slide 9: Step 2: A Specific Plan

Type:
Sorting

Description:
A specific plan gives the other person something concrete to answer. It should usually name an activity, keep the setting public or low-pressure, and make the next step easy to accept, adjust, or decline.

Instruction:
Context: you matched on a dating app, chatted for three days, and this would be your first meetup.

Categories:
- Too vague
- Too intense
- Balanced

Item:
"We should do something sometime."
Correct category: Too vague
Explanation:
There is no specific activity or next step to respond to.

Item:
"Would you want to grab coffee Saturday afternoon at the café near the park?"
Correct category: Balanced
Explanation:
The plan is specific, public, and easy to accept or decline.

Item:
"Want to spend the day together and see where things go?"
Correct category: Too intense
Explanation:
Too much time and commitment for a first meetup with someone you barely know.

Item:
"Would you be up for a 20-minute video call this week?"
Correct category: Balanced
Explanation:
Low-pressure, specific, and a reasonable first step before meeting.

Item:
"Come over to my apartment and we'll watch movies."
Correct category: Too intense
Explanation:
A private-home invitation is usually too much for a first app meetup.

Item:
"Maybe we could get food or something?"
Correct category: Too vague
Explanation:
The activity is unclear and there is no concrete plan.

Item:
"Would you like to meet for a short walk and coffee Sunday morning?"
Correct category: Balanced
Explanation:
Public, time-limited, and easy to leave if either person is uncomfortable.

Slide 10: Step 3: The Easy Out

Type: Sorting

Description:

A good easy out does two things at the same time:

- It makes it clear that the other person can say no.
- It makes it clear that you genuinely want to go on the date.

Too much pressure makes a yes feel less voluntary. Too much hedging can make it sound like you don't really want to ask in the first place. The goal is to land in the middle.

A quick note on confidence: Confidence is not acting like you will get a yes. Confidence is being clear about what you want while being okay with either answer.

Instruction:
Context: You have already made your ask.

Sort each response into:

Too much pressure
Too much hedging
Just right

Item:
"No pressure if not."
Correct category: Just right
Explanation:
Short, clear, and genuinely gives them room to decide.

Item:
"I really hope you'll say yes."
Correct category: Too much pressure
Explanation:
The focus shifts to managing your feelings rather than making a free choice.

Item:
"You probably aren't interested, but I thought I'd ask."
Correct category: Too much hedging
Explanation:
Sounds apologetic and assumes rejection before they have answered.

Item:
"No worries if you're not interested."
Correct category: Just right
Explanation:
Respects their choice without minimizing your own interest.

Item:
"I'd be pretty disappointed if you said no."
Correct category: Too much pressure
Explanation:
Creates guilt and makes a no harder to give honestly.

Item:
"Sorry, this is probably a weird question."
Correct category: Too much hedging
Explanation:
Apologizes for expressing interest instead of asking clearly.

Item:
"Feel free to say no if it's not your thing."
Correct category: Just right
Explanation:
Direct, respectful, and low-pressure.

Item:
"I know you're busy, probably not interested, and it's completely okay if this is a bad idea."
Correct category: Too much hedging
Explanation:
So many disclaimers that the invitation becomes unclear.

Item:
"I've really been hoping you'd say yes to this."

Correct category: Too much pressure

Explanation:
Adds emotional weight that can make the other person feel responsible for your reaction.

## Slide 11: Pick The Clear Ask

Type:
Multiple choice

Description: 
Now let's put all three steps together:

A warm signal that names the connection.
A specific first-date plan.
An easy out that respects the other person's choice.

The best asks are usually clear, specific, and low-pressure. They do not hide your interest, but they also do not require the other person to manage your feelings.

Instruction:
Choose the ask that gets the balance right.


Scenario 1

You have been talking after a weekly hobby group.

A "Want to hang out sometime?"

B "I've really enjoyed talking with you after group. Would you want to grab coffee Saturday afternoon? No pressure if not."

C "I think you're amazing and I've been wanting to ask you out for weeks. Please say yes."

Correct: B

Explanation:
B includes a warm signal, a specific plan, and a genuine easy out.

Scenario 2

You matched on a dating app and have been chatting for a week.

A "I've enjoyed talking with you about books. Would you want to meet for coffee this weekend? No worries if you're not interested."

B "Maybe we could do something if you want, but no worries if not, and sorry if this is weird."

C "We need to meet before this connection disappears."

Correct: A

Explanation:
A is clear and balanced. B hedges too much. C creates pressure.

Scenario 3

You know someone casually through mutual friends.

A "I like how easy you are to talk to. Would you want to get lunch this week? Feel free to say no."

B "Lunch?"

C "I know you're probably not interested, but I was wondering if maybe you'd want to do something sometime."

Correct: A

Explanation:
A clearly includes all three parts of the formula. B is too vague. C hides the ask behind assumptions and hedging.

Scenario 4

You matched on a dating app and have been joking about coffee shops.

A "I've liked our coffee shop debate. Want to try the quiet cafe near the park this weekend? No pressure if not."

B "We should test my coffee ranking system someday if you are not too busy and if that is not weird."

C "You need to experience my coffee standards in person."

Correct: A

Explanation:
A names the connection, gives a specific plan, and leaves room for a no. B has a fun idea, but it hedges and stays too vague. C sounds more forceful than invitational.

Scenario 5

You have been chatting with someone who said they prefer low-key plans.

A "Want to meet up or something?"

B "I like how easy this conversation has felt. Would you want to take a short walk and grab coffee Sunday morning? Totally okay if not."

C "I planned a whole afternoon for us because I think we will really click."

Correct: B

Explanation:
B is warm, specific, low-pressure, and aligned with a low-key preference. A is too vague and does not show you heard their preference. C is too intense for an early ask.
