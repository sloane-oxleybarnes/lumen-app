## Course Overview

### Title
Introducing Yourself to a New Colleague

### Short Description
Practice introducing yourself to a new colleague in a way that feels clear, warm enough, and useful, without overexplaining or leaving the other person guessing how to work with you.

### Confidence Question
How confident do you feel introducing yourself to a new colleague?

### Confidence Intro
In this course, Beckett will coach you through making a new work introduction feel less vague and less performative. You will practice saying who you are, what your role is, how you like to collaborate, and how to invite the other person into a smoother working rhythm.

### Reflective Question
What is one introduction line or collaboration preference you want to use or convey to a new colleague?

### Course Outcome
By the end, the user should have:
- A short intro they can use in Slack, email, or a meeting.
- A simple way to explain their role or contribution.
- One collaboration preference they can name without making the intro feel heavy.
- A way to ask how the other person likes to work.

## Section 1: What A Good Intro Actually Does

### Coach Framing
An introduction is not a performance. It is a small bridge.

When you meet a new colleague, the goal is not to sound impressive, perfectly casual, or instantly close. The goal is to give the other person enough context to know who you are, why you are in their work world, and how to start interacting with you.

A good intro usually answers four quiet questions:
- Who are you?
- Why are we connected?
- What should I know about working with you?
- What is the easiest next step?

You do not have to explain your whole personality, your diagnosis, your entire job history, or every reason you communicate the way you do. You are just opening a door.

### Key Points
- Clear is better than impressive.
- Warmth can be small.
- You do not need to disclose personal context to make collaboration easier.
- A useful intro helps both people understand what happens next.

### Example: Too Little
> Hi, nice to meet you.

**Why this can fall flat:**  
It is polite, but it does not give the other person enough context. They may not know what you do, why you are reaching out, or how to continue.

### Example: Too Much
> Hi Maya, I am Sloane. I know I can be a little intense when I am trying to understand a project, and I sometimes ask a lot of questions because I need everything to be clear, but I promise I am not trying to be annoying. I have had issues in the past where people misread me, so I just wanted to say that upfront.

**Why this can feel heavy:**  
It gives a lot of emotional context before the relationship has a frame. The other person may not know what they are supposed to do with that information.

### Example: Balanced
> Hi Maya, I am Sloane. I will be working with you on the beta onboarding flow. I am usually helpful with organizing messy pieces and spotting where users might get confused. What is the easiest way for us to stay aligned on this?

**Why this works:**  
It gives identity, context, contribution, and a next step. It is clear without overexplaining.

### Required Reflection
Pick the main goal for your introduction:
- I want to be friendly.
- I want to make collaboration smoother.
- I want to reduce awkwardness.
- I want to explain my role.
- I want to set expectations.
- I am introducing myself late and want to make it less awkward.

## Section 2: Your Strengths At Work

### Coach Framing
A work intro is easier when you lead with useful context, not apology.

Many neurodivergent people get used to explaining what might be difficult before naming what they bring. Beckett will help you flip that order. You can be honest about what helps you work well, but you do not have to start from a place of defending yourself.

Your strengths do not need to sound like corporate buzzwords. They just need to help the other person understand what kind of teammate you are.

### Strength Examples
- I notice patterns other people may miss.
- I am good at organizing scattered information.
- I ask clarifying questions early so we do not lose time later.
- I can be direct in a way that helps decisions move.
- I care about getting the details right.
- I am good at spotting where users, clients, or teammates might get confused.
- I can focus deeply when the goal is clear.
- I am thoughtful about tone and how something may land.

### What To Avoid
- Turning a strength into an apology.
- Listing too many strengths at once.
- Trying to sound like a performance review.
- Explaining a diagnosis when a practical work preference would be enough.

### Example: Apology Framing
> Sorry in advance, I ask a lot of questions because I sometimes have trouble understanding vague instructions.

### Example: Strength Framing
> I tend to ask clarifying questions early so I can make sure I am building toward the right thing.

### Required Activity: Build A Strength Line
Choose 2-3 strengths, then turn them into one intro sentence.

**Template:**  
I am usually helpful with [strength/contribution], especially when [work context].

**Example outputs:**
- I am usually helpful with organizing messy information, especially when a project has a lot of moving pieces.
- I am usually helpful with spotting unclear user flows, especially before something gets handed to a wider team.
- I tend to ask clarifying questions early so I can move faster once the direction is clear.

## Section 3: Warmth Without Overdoing It

### Coach Framing
Warmth does not have to mean being bubbly, informal, or emotionally exposed.

At work, warmth can be simple: use the person’s name, show you understand why you are connected, add one human sentence, and make the next step easy. That is enough.

If you are someone who worries about sounding cold, you may overcorrect by adding too many exclamation points, overexplaining your intentions, or trying to sound more casual than you feel. If you worry about sounding too eager, you may undercorrect and send something so short that the other person has no foothold.

Beckett’s default target here is direct and kind.

### Warmth Signals That Usually Work
- “Good to meet you.”
- “I am glad we will be working together.”
- “Thanks for looping me in.”
- “I am looking forward to learning how you like to work.”
- “Happy to compare notes.”

### Warmth Signals That Can Become Too Much
- “I am so excited, I have been really hoping we would get to work together.”
- “I promise I am easy to work with once you get used to me.”
- “Sorry if this is awkward.”
- “I know I am probably overthinking this.”

### Required Activity: Too Cold, Too Much, Or Balanced?

**Example 1:**  
> Hi. I am joining the project. Send me what I need.

Answer: Too cold.  
Why: It is direct, but it gives no warmth or collaboration signal. It may make the other person feel ordered around.

**Example 2:**  
> Hi Jordan!! I am honestly so relieved to be working with you because I have been nervous about this project and I really do best when people are patient with me, so thank you in advance.

Answer: Too much.  
Why: It asks for reassurance before the relationship has started. Some of this may be useful later, but it is heavy for a first intro.

**Example 3:**  
> Hi Jordan, I am Sloane. I will be helping with the launch checklist. Good to meet you, and happy to compare notes on how you want to split things up.

Answer: Balanced.  
Why: It is friendly, clear, and gives the other person a practical next step.

**Example 4:**  
> Hey Jordan, excited to work together. I am usually best with written next steps after we talk, so I may recap decisions in Slack as we go.

Answer: Balanced.  
Why: It names a preference lightly and makes it sound useful for the work, not like a burden.

## Section 4: Setting Collaboration Preferences

### Coach Framing
Preferences are not demands when you frame them as collaboration information.

You do not need to say, “I need you to communicate with me this exact way.” You can say, “Here is what helps me work well, and I would like to know what works for you too.”

That second half matters. Asking about their style makes the intro feel mutual instead of one-sided.

### Useful Preference Categories
- Written follow-up after meetings.
- Clear priorities when there are multiple tasks.
- Slack for quick questions, email for longer context.
- Direct feedback instead of vague hints.
- A heads-up when something is urgent.
- Time to process before giving a final answer.
- Examples or reference points when starting a new task.

### Preference Formula
**I tend to work best with [specific preference], especially when [situation]. What works best for you?**

### Examples
- I tend to work best with written next steps after a meeting, especially when there are multiple decisions. What works best for you?
- I am usually good with Slack for quick questions, but email is easier if there is a lot of context. How do you usually like to handle project updates?
- I do best when priorities are explicit, especially if several things are urgent at once. If something is highest priority, it helps me when that is named directly.

### What To Avoid
- “I am bad at vague communication.”
- “Please do not send unclear messages.”
- “I need you to be direct with me.”
- “I have ADHD/autism/dyslexia, so you need to…”

These may be true or partly true, but they can sound sharper than intended in a first intro. Beckett can help translate them into practical, neutral work language.

### Required Activity: Translate The Preference

**Raw thought:**  
I hate when people are vague.

**Work intro version:**  
I tend to work best when priorities and next steps are explicit.

**Raw thought:**  
I get overwhelmed when people throw a bunch of tasks at me at once.

**Work intro version:**  
If there are several moving pieces, it helps me to know what is most urgent first.

**Raw thought:**  
I need time to think before answering.

**Work intro version:**  
For bigger questions, I may take a little time to think and then come back with a clearer answer.

## Section 5: What Can Go Wrong

### Coach Framing
Most awkward introductions go sideways for one of two reasons: too little context or too much pressure.

Too little context leaves the other person unsure how to respond. Too much pressure makes them responsible for reassuring you, decoding you, or immediately adapting to you before they understand the work relationship.

The middle is usually best: clear, specific, and light.

### Common Traps

**Trap 1: The Vanishing Intro**  
You say “nice to meet you,” then disappear.  
Problem: The other person has no next step.

**Trap 2: The Defensive Intro**  
You explain all the ways people have misunderstood you before.  
Problem: The other person may feel like they are being warned before they have done anything.

**Trap 3: The Resume Intro**  
You list credentials, projects, or achievements.  
Problem: It may sound formal or self-protective instead of collaborative.

**Trap 4: The Over-Casual Mask**  
You try to sound breezy or funny in a way that does not feel like you.  
Problem: It can make the interaction more draining and less clear.

**Trap 5: The Hidden Need**  
You have a real work preference but do not name it, then get frustrated later when the other person does not guess it.  
Problem: The other person cannot work with information they do not have.

### Required Activity: Fix The Intro

**Original:**  
> Hey, I am Sloane. I am joining this project. I sometimes ask a lot of questions, but I promise I am not trying to be difficult. I have had people get annoyed with me before, so I just wanted to say that.

**What needs fixing:**
- It starts with anxiety instead of context.
- It asks the colleague to reassure the user.
- It explains past pain before explaining the current collaboration.

**Better version:**  
> Hi, I am Sloane. I will be helping with the onboarding flow. I tend to ask clarifying questions early so I can make sure I am solving the right problem. Good to meet you, and happy to sync on how you want to split the work.

## Section 6: Choose The Channel

### Coach Framing
The same introduction changes depending on where it happens.

A Slack intro can be shorter. An email intro may need more context. A meeting intro should be easy to say out loud. A project kickoff intro can include more collaboration preferences because the work relationship is starting immediately.

Beckett will help you choose the right amount of information for the channel.

### Slack / Teams DM
Best when:
- You need a quick, friendly intro.
- You are starting a project together.
- You want to open the door without making it formal.

Example:
> Hi Maya, I am Sloane. I will be helping with the beta onboarding flow. Good to meet you. What is the easiest way for us to stay aligned on this?

### Email
Best when:
- The person needs more context.
- There are several people involved.
- You want a written record.

Example:
> Hi Maya,  
>  
> I am Sloane, and I will be helping with the beta onboarding flow. I am usually helpful with organizing messy pieces and spotting where users might get confused.  
>  
> I tend to work best with clear written next steps after a discussion, so I may recap decisions as we go. What is the easiest way for us to stay aligned?

### Meeting / In Person
Best when:
- You are being introduced live.
- The intro needs to be short and easy to say.
- You can follow up later in writing.

Example:
> I am Sloane. I will be helping with onboarding and user flow. I am usually useful when there are a lot of moving pieces to organize, and I am looking forward to working with you.

### New Project Kickoff
Best when:
- You are starting collaboration immediately.
- You need to set expectations early.
- There may be multiple work styles in the group.

Example:
> I am Sloane. I will be focused on onboarding and user confusion points. I tend to work best when decisions and next steps are written down, so I may recap what we decide after meetings. I would also love to know how each of you prefers to handle quick questions versus bigger updates.

### Required Activity: Pick A Channel
User chooses:
- Slack / Teams
- Email
- Meeting / in person
- New project kickoff
- Not sure

Then Beckett shows a recommended intro length:
- Slack: 2-3 sentences.
- Email: 1 short paragraph plus a question.
- Meeting: 1-2 sentences.
- Kickoff: role + contribution + preference + group question.

## Section 7: Build Your Intro

### Coach Framing
Now we will build your intro from pieces. You do not need to make it perfect on the first try.

The safest structure is:
1. Greeting.
2. Name.
3. Work context.
4. Useful contribution.
5. Collaboration preference or next step.
6. Question back to them.

### Template
> Hi [Name], I am [Your Name]. I will be working with you on [context]. I am usually helpful with [strength/contribution]. I tend to work best with [collaboration preference], especially when [situation]. What works best for you?

### Required Draft Prompts
The user fills:
- Person’s name:
- Work context:
- My role or contribution:
- One strength:
- One collaboration preference:
- Question I want to ask them:

### Generated Draft Options

**Short version:**  
> Hi [Name], I am [Your Name]. I will be working with you on [context]. I am usually helpful with [strength]. Good to meet you.

**Warm version:**  
> Hi [Name], I am [Your Name]. I am glad we will be working together on [context]. I am usually helpful with [strength], and I would love to learn how you like to stay aligned on projects like this.

**Structured version:**  
> Hi [Name], I am [Your Name]. I will be focused on [role/context]. I tend to work best with [preference], especially when [situation]. What is the easiest way for us to coordinate?

## Section 8: Practice With Beckett

### Coach Framing
Practice is where you try the words before the real interaction.

Beckett will play the new colleague. The goal is not to “win” the conversation. The goal is to practice being clear, warm enough, and specific.

### Scenario Setup
Default colleague:
- Name: Maya
- Role: Cross-functional teammate
- Context: You are both joining a beta onboarding project.
- Communication style: Friendly, efficient, and a little busy.

### Practice Goals
User chooses one:
- Introduce myself.
- Explain my role.
- Ask how they like to work.
- Mention a collaboration preference.
- Recover from a late or awkward intro.

### Beckett Persona Prompt
You are Maya, a cross-functional colleague who has just been introduced to the user on a workplace project. You are friendly, busy, and practical. You want to understand who the user is, what they are working on, and how to collaborate without making the exchange too long.

Keep responses realistic for workplace Slack or email. Do not be overly enthusiastic. If the user is clear and collaborative, respond warmly and give a useful next step. If the user is vague, ask a simple clarifying question. If the user overshares or apologizes heavily, respond kindly but redirect toward the work context.

Never break character. You are Maya, not Beckett.

### Help Me Start Options
Before the first message, Beckett offers:

**Direct:**  
> Hi Maya, I am Sloane. I will be working with you on the onboarding flow. Good to meet you.

**Warm:**  
> Hi Maya, I am Sloane. I am glad we will be working together on onboarding. I would love to learn how you like to stay aligned on projects like this.

**Structured:**  
> Hi Maya, I am Sloane. I will be focused on user confusion points in the onboarding flow. I tend to work best with written next steps, so I may recap decisions as we go.

### Feedback Rules
Beckett should coach toward:
- Clearer context.
- Less apologizing.
- More specific next steps.
- Warmer phrasing when the message sounds too abrupt.
- Shorter phrasing when the message becomes too much.

## Section 9: Final Draft

### Coach Framing
The best final draft is one you would actually send.

It does not need to include everything from the course. Choose the pieces that fit the person, channel, and situation. If you are unsure, use the shorter version. You can always add more once the conversation has started.

### Required Final Draft
User creates:
- Short intro.
- Warmer intro.
- Structured intro.

### Final Checklist
Before you send, check:
- Did I say who I am?
- Did I explain why I am reaching out?
- Did I include one useful work context point?
- Did I avoid apologizing for existing?
- Did I give them an easy way to respond?
- Did I keep sensitive personal context optional?

### Final Example
> Hi Maya, I am Sloane. I will be working with you on the beta onboarding flow. I am usually helpful with organizing messy pieces and spotting where users might get confused. I tend to work best when next steps are written down after we talk, so I may recap decisions in Slack as we go. What is the easiest way for us to stay aligned on this?
